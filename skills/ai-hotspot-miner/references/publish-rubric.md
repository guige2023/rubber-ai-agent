# Publication Rubric

Use this rubric when the user wants content that earns attention, traffic, follows, or discussion.

## 1. Topic Selection

Pick the topic with the best combination of:

- `Newness`: genuinely recent, not recycled old news
- `Impact`: affects workflows, product strategy, model choice, cost, or market structure
- `Clarity`: easy to summarize in one sentence
- `Tension`: contains contrast, surprise, conflict, speed, or risk
- `Audience Fit`: clearly matters to the user's intended readers

If no topic scores well across these dimensions, switch to a concise roundup instead of forcing a hero story.

## 2. Article Strategy

Before drafting, decide explicitly:

- `Core Claim`: what is the one thing the reader should remember?
- `Reader Payoff`: what does the reader gain by spending time on this piece?
- `Hook Type`: surprise, consequence, conflict, status shift, practical implication, or pattern reveal
- `Narrative Shape`: single-story deep dive or multi-signal roundup

If the underlying research only supports a weak or fragmented hero topic, prefer a roundup. Do not force a deep dive when the evidence is better as a cluster.

## 3. Title Guidance

Good titles usually combine a concrete entity with a reason to click.

Prefer:

- specific names over generic categories
- consequences over labels
- concrete stakes over abstract praise

Be wary of:

- hype that exceeds the evidence
- curiosity without a clear payoff
- framings the article body cannot really support

Produce title options that vary by click driver, not just by intensity:

- `Consequence-led`: highlights what changes
- `Contrast-led`: highlights strategic reversal or tension
- `Reframing-led`: highlights what actually matters beneath the surface
- `Reader-stakes-led`: highlights why this audience should care now

The title should make the payoff legible. A reader should feel not just curiosity, but expected value from clicking.

## 4. Draft Structure

For a single-topic article, prefer:

1. `Lead`: 2-4 sentences that name the event and the reason it matters
2. `What Happened`: the concrete facts
3. `Why It Matters`: the key implication for the audience
4. `What To Watch`: the next-order consequence, uncertainty, or opportunity
5. `Close`: one crisp takeaway, judgment, or question worth discussing

For a roundup article, prefer:

1. `Lead`: today's meta-pattern
2. `Signal 1`
3. `Signal 2`
4. `Signal 3`
5. `Close`: what the cluster says about the market

## 5. Quality Bar

The article should make at least one strong move in each category:

- `Information`: teach the reader something real
- `Judgment`: help the reader interpret the signal
- `Compression`: remove fluff and duplicated facts
- `Memorability`: include one line or framing the reader may want to quote or forward
- `Epistemic Clarity`: make it clear what is fact, what is interpretation, and what is still emerging

## 6. Evidence Discipline

- Every important claim should trace back to a source in the research brief.
- Mark estimates or inferences clearly.
- If a signal is early or weak, say so directly.
- If the research brief shows low confidence or unresolved contradiction, the draft should explicitly narrow its claim instead of writing with false certainty.

## 7. Chinese Publishing Finish

For Chinese deliverables:

- remove non-essential spaces between Chinese and adjacent English or numbers,
- keep literal formatting only when it preserves model names, commands, paths, URLs, or direct quotes,
- make the final copy read like native Chinese editorial writing, not a lightly localized English draft.
